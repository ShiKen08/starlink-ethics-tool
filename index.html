What Happened

Kwanda secured the first data sovereignty concession from SpaceX in Africa: an 8% revenue share and a read-only data cache. No legal jurisdiction. No exit clause. Other nations followed — using Kwanda's deal as the template. SpaceX learned something too: the ceiling is low.

Post-Colonial Ethics Analysis

Fanon (1961) described this as the "negotiating table trap": the colonised nation celebrates access to the table, while the coloniser sets the menu. The structural power — who owns the satellites, who writes the code, who stores the data — remained unchanged.

Negotiation without alternatives is not sovereignty. It is the best deal available to a party with no leverage.

Rodney (1972) in How Europe Underdeveloped Africa described this pattern: economic growth that deepens structural dependency. The colony grows richer in absolute terms while becoming more bound to the metropole.

Kwanda's GDP rose. Its sovereignty fell. Modernisation theory calls this development. Dependency theory calls it extraction with extra steps.

This is a real dilemma faced by real nations right now.

If you adopted Starlink:

  • 2.4 million people came online
  • Schools and hospitals connected
  • But infrastructure is owned in California
  • One man can disconnect your nation
  • Your data flows under US law, not yours
  • Exit costs make leaving nearly impossible

If you rejected Starlink:

  • Your sovereignty remained intact — formally
  • 2.4 million people stayed offline
  • Rural hospitals stayed isolated
  • The cost is borne by the most vulnerable
  • Not by the ministers who made the choice
  • The alternative never materialised

Post-Colonial Ethics Analysis

Couldry & Mejias (2019) argue this is not a new dilemma — it is the same logic as colonial railways: infrastructure presented as development, designed for extraction. The choice between adoption and rejection is itself a product of the conditions colonialism created. Nations in the Global South did not create the capital deficit that makes sovereign alternatives impossible.

Nkrumah (1965) called this neo-colonialism: "the last stage of imperialism" — formal independence with structural dependency. Starlink's expansion follows the same logic as the telegraph and the railway before it. The infrastructure is real. The benefit is real. The control is real too.

There is no clean answer.

This is a real dilemma faced by real nations right now.

If you adopted Starlink:

  • 2.4 million people came online
  • Schools and hospitals connected
  • But infrastructure is owned in California
  • One man can disconnect your nation
  • Your data flows under US law, not yours
  • Switching costs make exit nearly impossible

If you rejected Starlink:

  • Your sovereignty remained intact — formally
  • 2.4 million people stayed offline
  • Rural hospitals stayed isolated
  • The cost is borne by the most vulnerable
  • Not by the ministers who made the choice
  • The alternative never materialised

Post-Colonial Ethics Analysis

Couldry & Mejias (2019) argue this is not a new dilemma — it is the same logic as colonial railways: infrastructure presented as development, designed for extraction. The choice between adoption and rejection is itself a product of the conditions colonialism created.

Nkrumah (1965) called this neo-colonialism: formal independence with structural dependency. Nations in the Global South did not create the capital deficit that makes sovereign alternatives impossible.
